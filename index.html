Abstract

Lung cancer causes over 150,000 casualties every year, making it the deadliest of all of the cancers. It has become crucial to design a tool to successfully diagnose this disease at an earlier stage because this would allow the mortality rate to decrease significantly. The current screening and diagnostic methods are invasive, not cost effective and cumbersome to use. Our prototype breathalyzer uses two nanosensors to distinguish the slight differences in concentrations between two volatile organic compounds, acetone and toluene. The ratio of concentrations between these two gases can be used to deduce whether this patient has lung cancer or not. While our prototype improves upon the drawbacks of the current technologies, including x-rays, bone scans, PET-CT scans, low-dose CT scans, and biopsies, it does have some limitations of its own. The most prominent drawback of our system is its inability to identify the stage of lung cancer.
